What is Air Pollution?

Air Pollution

Air pollution is the contamination of the indoor or outdoor environment by chemical, physical, or biological agents that modify the natural characteristics of the atmosphere. Common sources include household combustion devices, motor vehicles, industrial facilities, and forest fires. Pollutants of major public health concern, such as particulate matter, carbon monoxide, ozone, nitrogen dioxide, and sulfur dioxide, cause respiratory and other diseases, making air pollution a significant source of morbidity and mortality worldwide. According to WHO data, almost all of the global population (99%) breathes air that exceeds WHO guideline limits and contains high levels of pollutants, with the highest exposures occurring in low- and middle-income countries.

The issue of air quality is closely linked to the earth's climate and ecosystems globally. Importantly, many of the drivers of air pollution, like the combustion of fossil fuels, are also sources of greenhouse gas emissions. Therefore, policies aimed at reducing air pollution offer a win-win strategy for both climate and human health. These measures lower the burden of disease attributable to air pollution while also contributing to the near- and long-term mitigation of climate change.

Air Pollution Impacts

Health Impact

Air pollution poses a major threat to health. Ambient air pollution causes fine particulate matter which results in strokes, heart diseases, lung cancer, and acute and chronic respiratory diseases. The combined effects of ambient and household air pollution are associated with 7 million premature deaths annually.

Household Air Pollution

Around 2.4 billion people are exposed to dangerous levels of household air pollution. This exposure occurs while using polluting open fires or simple stoves for cooking fuelled by kerosene, biomass (wood, animal dung and crop waste), and coal, which significantly contributes to the global health burden.

Sources and Solutions

Sources of air pollution are multiple. The major outdoor pollution sources include residential energy for cooking and heating, vehicles, power generation, and industry. Policies and investments that support sustainable land use, cleaner household energy and transport, and better municipal waste management can reduce key sources of ambient air pollution.

Air Pollution Effects in the World

Global Air Quality Challenges

This chart shows global death rates from air pollution between 1990 and 2021. It covers three sources: outdoor ozone pollution, outdoor particulate pollution, and indoor air pollution from fuels. In 1990, the rate was about 180 deaths per 100,000 people, but it fell to nearly 100 by 2021, showing clear progress.

Indoor air pollution was the main cause in the 1990s but dropped sharply as cleaner cooking and heating methods spread. Outdoor particulate pollution also declined but remains dangerous, as tiny particles damage the lungs and heart. Outdoor ozone pollution is smaller but has stayed almost constant over time.

Overall, the chart shows big improvements, especially indoors, but outdoor pollution is still a serious challenge. Stronger policies to cut emissions and expand clean energy are needed to protect health worldwide. Air pollution remains one of the biggest global health threats, and progress must continue to ensure safe air for everyone.

Health Impact

Air pollution is linked to respiratory diseases, cardiovascular problems, and increased risk of stroke. Children and the elderly are particularly vulnerable to its effects.

Economic Costs

The global economic cost of air pollution is estimated at over $5 trillion annually, including healthcare expenses and lost productivity.

Environmental Damage

Air pollution contributes to acid rain, damages ecosystems, reduces crop yields, and accelerates climate change through greenhouse gas emissions.

Air Pollution Effects in Egypt

Egypt's Air Quality Challenges

The pie chart shows the status of air quality in different levels. The largest share, 38%, is in the "Emergency" category, which means the air was extremely polluted and unsafe. Another 25% falls under "Alert," also showing dangerous pollution levels.

At the same time, 25% of the readings were "Normal," where the air quality was acceptable, and 12% were in the "Attention" category, which means pollution was moderate but still concerning.

No data was recorded for "Clean," "Moderate," or "Warning," all standing at 0%. This highlights that safe and fresh air was almost absent, while most of the time air pollution was harmful.

Urban Centers

Cairo and other major cities experience high levels of pollution due to traffic congestion and industrial activities. The high population density and aging vehicle fleet contribute significantly to poor air quality in urban areas.

Agricultural Impact

Seasonal burning of agricultural waste, particularly rice straw, contributes significantly to air pollution, especially during specific months. This practice releases large amounts of particulate matter and harmful gases into the atmosphere.

Health Consequences

Respiratory illnesses have increased in areas with poor air quality, affecting vulnerable populations the most. Children, the elderly, and those with pre-existing conditions face the highest risk from prolonged exposure to polluted air.

What is the meaning of Air Quality Index?

Air Quality Index

The Air Quality Index, or AQI, is a standardized tool used to report how clean or polluted the air currently is and what associated health effects might be a concern. It functions like a thermometer, but instead of showing temperature, it shows levels of major air pollutants regulated by the Clean Air Act, including ground-level ozone, particle pollution, carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, and nitrogen dioxide. The AQI translates complex air quality data into a simple color-coded scale, making it easy for the public to understand the state of their air at a glance.


The AQI scale is divided into color-coded categories that range from 0 to 500. A low AQI value, represented by green (0-50), means air quality is good, while a high AQI value, represented by maroon (301-500), signifies hazardous conditions. Each category corresponds to a different level of health concern. For example, when the AQI is in the "unhealthy for sensitive groups" range, people with asthma or heart conditions may be at risk, while an "unhealthy" or "hazardous" AQI warns that everyone may experience serious health effects.
